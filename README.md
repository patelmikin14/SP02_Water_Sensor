**SP02 Sensor**

The first thing I did was get comfortable with the environment understanding the pre-installed component libraries, how symbols and power symbols work, and what a well-structured schematic actually looks like before touching the software. I reviewed the full circuit on paper first, mapped out all the requirements, and then rebuilt it from scratch in KiCad. Every connection, every component value, every net placed and verified manually. That foundation set the tone for everything that followed.

One habit I picked up early: run the Electrical Rules Check (ERC) frequently while building not just at the end. Catching errors right after adding a component is orders of magnitude faster than chasing them down in a finished schematic. I also got familiar with net labels and hierarchical mapping to keep the design clean and easy to reconfigure. Messy schematics are a silent time-killer.

The OLED module we planned to use wasn't in the KiCad library not even close. So, I built a custom symbol from scratch, mapping all the pins correctly, defining the data transfer pathways, and making sure it was compatible with the rest of the board architecture. Creating custom parts sounds intimidating at first, but it forces you to actually read and understand the datasheet rather than just trust a library entry someone else made.

Once the schematic was locked, every part needed a physical footprint the actual size and land pattern that lands on the board. Out of thousands of library options, I had to find the best match for each unique part, cross-referencing distributor availability, shipping feasibility, mechanical drawings, and datasheet dimensions. It was tedious. But this step is where a design moves from theoretically correct to actually manufacturable, and it's also where you can meaningfully optimize cost. Getting the balance right between quality, price, availability, and real-world workability is its own skill.

Two components both custom had no suitable footprint in the library. I used the KiCad footprint editor to build them out completely: pad definitions, through-hole geometry, courtyard, silkscreen labels, the works. Nothing about it was guesswork every dimension came from mechanical drawings and manufacturer specs.

Before placing a single trace, I picked the manufacturer and went through every single tolerance value they published minimum trace width, clearance, via drill size, copper weight and understood what each number actually meant for the design. Most experienced designers would pull these from a previous project. I went the long route on purpose. It made every constraint in the layout feel grounded in a real-world reason rather than an arbitrary rule.

Clearing the rat’s nest and turning a web of unconnected nets into clean, intentional traces is genuinely satisfying. I learned how vias fit into the routing strategy and why the type of connection matters as much as the connection itself particularly for designs that need to scale to higher-volume manufacturing. I also got into the layer stack up, understanding what each layer contributes and how they interact in the final physical board.

After routing, I added component designators, values, the board logo, and manufacturer markings then ran the Design Rules Check. DRC is the last line of defense before files leave your hands. It validates everything against the manufacturer's tolerances and catches the kind of errors that would be expensive or impossible to fix post-fabrication. If ERC is the schematic's safety net, DRC is the layouts. Both deserve real attention.

The final step was generating the Bill of Materials and Gerber files the package a manufacturer actually uses to build your board. Different manufacturers have different file format requirements, which is something worth checking before you start generating files. Once exported, the Gerbers can be uploaded directly to the manufacturer's portal to configure board specs, finish options, and quantities. Seeing the 3D render of the board after uploading is a good moment.
